# Introduction Writing Guide for Renewable Energy Papers

Load `../_shared/core/narrative-principles.md` with this guide.

## Goal

Write an introduction that turns evidence into a clear research problem.

## Logic Map

```text
Energy-system decision or phenomenon
to field-level tension
to why existing work leaves the tension unresolved
to the paper's question
to evidence design
to contribution preview
```

## Backward Reasoning

Answer these before drafting:

1. What should the reader believe or decide differently after the paper?
2. What evidence supports that change?
3. Which prior assumption, limitation, or unresolved problem makes the paper
   necessary?
4. What boundary prevents the claim from becoming too broad?

## Forward Structure

1. Open with the energy decision, system constraint, or scientific tension.
2. Summarize relevant practice or literature only as needed to expose the gap.
3. State why the gap is difficult.
4. Present the paper's question and evidence design.
5. Preview contribution types as the knowledge the paper is designed to
   produce.

## Introduction Spoiler Control

Introduction may preview contribution types, but should avoid reporting
detailed empirical outcomes, numerical results, final method rankings, operating
boundary conclusions, or operational recommendations that belong to Results or
Discussion. Use research-task language before evidence is presented.

## Generic Opening Skeletons

### Application-to-tension

```text
[Energy decision] relies on [model, measurement, control, planning, or market
procedure]. Yet [unresolved limitation] means that better technical performance
does not automatically translate into better [operation, planning, reliability,
cost, safety, or sustainability].
```

### Assumption-to-diagnosis

```text
A common assumption in [field] is that [accepted intuition]. This assumption is
plausible under [setting], but it remains unclear whether [mechanism or
boundary] holds when [energy-system condition] changes.
```

### Method-to-boundary

```text
Recent methods have improved [metric or capability], but their operating
boundary is less clear. In practice, [decision maker] needs to know when the
method helps, when it fails, and what evidence distinguishes these regimes.
```

## Contribution Style

Introduction contribution bullets should state what the paper is designed to
teach without giving the full result:

```text
1. We develop a diagnostic framework for identifying [mechanism or boundary].
2. We examine whether [moderator or condition] changes the value of [method,
   design, or policy alternative].
3. We translate the resulting evidence into [selection, design, or deployment]
   framework.
```

Results and Discussion can use finding language after evidence is presented:

```text
1. We find that [observed pattern].
2. The evidence shows that [supported interpretation].
3. This implies that [bounded consequence].
```

Weak contribution bullets only list activities:

```text
1. We conduct experiments.
2. We compare methods.
3. We draw figures.
```

## Quality Checklist

1. Does the first page contain a field-level tension?
2. Is the paper's question stated before the contribution list?
3. Are contributions phrased as knowledge gained?
4. Are experiment details delayed until they support the question?
5. Are evidence boundaries visible without reducing the paper's importance?
6. Does the Introduction avoid detailed numerical results, final method
   rankings, operating-boundary conclusions, and operational recommendations?
